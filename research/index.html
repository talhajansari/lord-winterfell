Curious cases of the SR830 Lock-In Amplifiers

A lock in amplifier is a critical component in scientific research. The CERN experiments, for example, use hundreds of these instruments. The implications, therefore, are large if a particular lock-in amplifier diverges from its expected behavior. The SR830 Digital Lock-In Amplifier by SRS, one of the pioneers in the digitized lock-in amplification, showed signs of such peculiarity.

In my Physics thesis, under supervision of Prof. Jeffrey S. Dunham, I designed and conducted experiments to document and understand these deviations. The research had three major components. First, I studied the theoretical basis of lock-in amplification (phase-sensitive multiplication) and the algorithms used in the SR830 model. Second, I designed and built a comprehensive LabVIEW program to conduct the experiment with minimal human interaction. This was necessary as each run took 4 to 5 days to complete, and needed an automatic troubleshooting and recovery mechanism in case of equipment failures (which were quite often). Third, I built a theoretical lock-in amplifier using the computational power of Mathematica, and compared it with the actual data gathered from the SRS830 experiments. Comparisons revealed that under certain settings, the SR830 output showed oscillatory deviations from its theoretical output, as shown in the resonance plots presented below.


Abstract: The project documents and explores the oscillatory deviations of the SR830 lock-in amplifier output from a Butterworth-filter based theoretical model. A LabVIEW program is developed for experiment control and data acquisition. Resonance response plots at the reference frequency of 32 765.00 Hz at time constants of 1 s, 10 s, and 30 s and filter roll-off settings of 6, 12, 18, and 24 dB/octave, are obtained and compared with the resonance plots of an ideal butter-worth filter. We find that there is a strong evidence of non-mitigating oscillatary deviation of the SR830 output from its theory at 30 s time constant and 24 dB/octave filter roll-off. We further find that the oscillations in the SR830 are periodic, comprising of several frequencies close to the harmonics of the reference and the lock-in amplifier input frequency.
